Shared Ownership explainer

Why increasing ownership % can change your monthly cost

← Back to the calculator

The simple idea

In shared ownership, you usually pay two things each month:

  • Mortgage payment on the share you own (this can include interest + capital repayment if you choose repayment).
  • Rent on the share you don’t own (this is typically like “interest-only” — it doesn’t reduce a mortgage balance because you don’t own that part yet).

So your monthly cost isn’t just “mortgage vs mortgage” — it’s mortgage + rent.

Why rent feels like “interest-only”

With a normal repayment mortgage, part of your payment reduces the amount you owe (capital repayment). Over time, that means: you build equity and your interest portion typically shrinks.

Rent on the unowned share is different: it’s a fee for using the part of the property owned by the housing provider/landlord. Paying rent doesn’t pay down a loan balance in your name for that share — so it can feel like an “interest-only” component.

Layman example (same home, different ownership)

This is an educational example to build intuition. Real offers vary by lender, scheme rules, service charges, fees, and rent review clauses.

Home value
£400,000
Mortgage rate / term
4.5% / 30 years
Deposit on owned share
10%
Rent rate on unowned share (annual)
2.7%

Scenario A: You own 50%

  • You mortgage the owned half (after deposit).
  • You pay rent on the unowned half.

Scenario B: You own 75%

  • You mortgage a bigger owned share (after deposit) → mortgage payment usually increases.
  • You pay rent on a smaller unowned share → rent usually decreases.

Whether your total monthly outpay goes up or down depends on how these two parts trade off: mortgage interest rate vs rent rate, term, deposit, and the ownership %.

Pros and cons of higher ownership %

Higher ownership (e.g., 75% vs 50%)

  • Pros: Less rent exposure (rent is charged on a smaller unowned share).
  • Pros: More of your monthly payment can go toward building equity (if you’re on a repayment mortgage).
  • Cons: You usually need a larger mortgage (and/or deposit), increasing affordability pressure.
  • Cons: You take on more exposure to interest-rate changes on a larger loan amount.

Lower ownership (e.g., 25%–50%)

  • Pros: Smaller mortgage needed upfront, which may improve initial affordability.
  • Pros: Lower deposit requirement in absolute £ terms (because it’s applied to a smaller owned share).
  • Cons: Higher rent exposure (charged on a larger unowned share), and rent doesn’t reduce a loan balance.
  • Cons: You may build equity more slowly (because a bigger part of your monthly outgo is rent).

Important disclaimers

  • This page is for information only and does not constitute financial advice.
  • Shared ownership often includes other costs not modelled here (e.g., service charges, insurance, fees).
  • Rent may increase over time (for example, linked to inflation or scheme rules).
  • Different lenders and schemes have different eligibility and affordability assessments.

Always check the specific shared ownership lease terms and speak to a qualified adviser if needed.
